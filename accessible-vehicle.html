Accessible Ride-On Vehicle

The Mission

Pitt Accessible 4 All (A4A) is a student engineering organization at the University of Pittsburgh that builds custom adaptive ride-on vehicles for children with physical disabilities. The program pairs engineering students with pediatric users and their families to design modifications that allow children who cannot operate standard ride-on toys to experience independent mobility — often for the first time.

As Project Co-Lead and Engineer, I lead the team responsible for a young boy with Spina Bifida L4-L5, resulting in paraplegia and significantly reduced proprioception in his lower extremities. The standard ride-on vehicle he loved was inaccessible to him. Our job was to change that.

Design Priority: Every modification had to be driven by the user's specific needs, abilities, and preferences — not by what was easiest to build. Engineering in service of the user, not the other way around.

Understanding the User

Before designing anything, we worked closely with the child and his family to understand his exact physical capabilities, what he loved about the vehicle, and what barriers prevented him from using it independently. Two core constraints shaped everything:

  • Paraplegia: He has no functional use of his lower limbs, making the standard foot-pedal accelerator completely inaccessible.
  • Low proprioception: Reduced sensory feedback in his extremities meant any alternative control had to be simple, reliable, and require minimal fine motor precision to operate safely.

Engineering Modifications

We designed and implemented three distinct categories of modifications to the vehicle:

🕹️
Accelerator Conversion

Replaced the foot-pedal accelerator with a hand-accessible togglable button mounted within easy reach. The toggle design — press once to go, press again to stop — was selected over a hold-to-operate design specifically because it reduces the sustained grip and fine motor control required, accommodating his low proprioception.

🚒
Vehicle Replication & Customization

The child's favorite vehicle model was a fire truck. Using CriCut vinyl cutting and Bambu 3D printing, we replicated the aesthetic details of his chosen model — decals, markings, and body graphics — to ensure the vehicle looked exactly like what he wanted, not like a medical device.

🚨
Embedded Feature Systems

Designed and wired custom embedded systems to add functional features that matched the vehicle identity: an operational siren, working emergency lights, and a deployable hose prop. These were built using embedded circuitry integrated into the vehicle body, making the experience immersive and engaging for the user.

What This Project Taught Me

Every engineering project I had done before A4A had an abstract end user. This one did not. The child we built for sat across from us at our first meeting and told us what he wanted. That changes the way you think about every design decision — because you are not optimizing for a specification, you are optimizing for a person.

The most important engineering lesson this project reinforced is that accessibility is not a constraint you work around — it is the design brief. The toggle accelerator was not a compromise from the standard pedal; it was a better solution for this user. Designing for specific human needs pushes you toward more thoughtful, precise engineering than open-ended projects often do.

This project is ongoing. As the child grows and his needs evolve, so will the vehicle.
